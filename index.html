FERROUS · SAMPLE 01 Eutectoid Steel (Fe-0.8 wt% C)

Alloy Name Fe-C Eutectoid Steel
Composition (wt%) Fe - 0.8% C
Processing / Treatment Slow cooled from the austenite region
Etching Details Nital (90% ethanol, 10% nitric acid); immersed ~5 seconds
Microstructure Description Long, alternating parallel strips resembling elongated bacteria, arranged in colonies oriented in different directions. The lamellae are closely spaced and parallel within each colony. Apparent spacing changes between colonies depending on the angle of the lamellae relative to the polished surface.
Phases Present
  • PearliteThe dominant and only microconstituent - a lamellar composite of ferrite and cementite.
  • Ferrite (a-Fe)Light/white lamellae; soft and ductile.
  • Cementite (Fe₃C)Dark lamellae; hard and brittle.
Why It Looks Like That This steel has the eutectoid composition (0.8 wt% C). Slow cooling drives the eutectoid reaction γ → α + Fe₃C, producing pearlite. Cementite nucleates first at austenite grain boundaries, depleting adjacent regions of carbon and favouring ferrite nucleation alongside it - this alternating nucleation advances the pearlite nodule into the austenite. Because the sample was slowly cooled, the degree of undercooling is small, giving a low carbide nucleation rate and a large interlamellar spacing: a coarse pearlite microstructure. The interlamellar spacing is inversely proportional to undercooling. Each colony consists of two interpenetrating single crystals of ferrite and cementite with defined crystallographic orientation relationships.

Composition (wt%)Fe - 30% Ni, 0.3% C
Processing / TreatmentQuenched to -80°C
Etching Details3% Nital
Microstructure Description Lenticular (lens-shaped) plates scattered throughout the matrix, resembling broken glass shards or jagged needles criss-crossing in multiple directions. Each plate shows a characteristic pointed, tapered shape.
Phases Present
  • Plate MartensiteDominant metastable phase - lenticular, needle/plate-like features formed by a diffusionless shear transformation.
  • Retained AustenitePossibly present between martensitic plates - transformation may be incomplete.

Alloy NameHypereutectic Grey Cast Iron
Composition (wt%)Fe - 3.6% C, 2.1% Si
Processing / TreatmentCast and slowly cooled
Etching DetailsNital etching
Microstructure Description Long, thick, irregular strand-like features resembling tangled coarse hairs scattered throughout the matrix. These are interconnected graphite flakes embedded in a complex surrounding matrix of transformed ledeburite.
Phases Present
  • Graphite FlakesDark, elongated, interconnected free carbon - primary phase to solidify from the melt.
  • PearliteLamellar ferrite + cementite forming below the eutectoid temperature.
  • Cementite (Fe₃C)Hard carbide within the transformed ledeburite.
  • Trans. LedeburiteOriginal austenite + cementite eutectic mixture, subsequently transformed on cooling.
Why It Looks Like That C equivalent (4.37 wt%) exceeds the eutectic (4.3 wt%), making this a hypereutectic grey iron. Silicon strongly promotes graphite over cementite: free graphite solidifies first directly from the melt as flakes - not dendrites - aligning in the heat-flow direction. The remaining liquid forms ledeburite (austenite + cementite eutectic). The austenite transforms by the eutectoid reaction (γ → α + Fe₃C), producing the pearlite and cementite matrix. Graphite flakes compensate for solidification shrinkage but act as internal crack-like stress concentrators, making the iron brittle despite its good compressive strength.

The overall hard and brittle character arises from the large volume fraction of Fe₃C throughout.

Part III - Comparison and Answers to Questions

A. Ferrous Alloys

Carbon content and cooling rate are the two dominant variables in ferrous alloys. At the eutectoid composition (0.8 wt% C), slow cooling produces coarse pearlite - lamellar spacing inversely proportional to undercooling. Above 0.8 wt% C, proeutectoid cementite forms first at grain boundaries; fast cooling yields Widmanstätten cementite plates. Quenching bypasses the pearlite nose entirely, producing the diffusionless martensitic transformation - FCC austenite shears to BCT martensite, with lenticular plates growing at near-sonic velocity. At very high carbon contents (cast irons), the eutectic reaction governs solidification: silicon content determines whether graphite flakes form (grey iron - brittle, crack-like flakes) or all carbon stays as cementite (white iron - extremely hard). Ni-hard iron leverages Ni to suppress pearlite and enforce martensite, with Cr hardening the carbides.

B. Non-Ferrous Alloys

In brass (Cu-Zn), zinc content governs phase balance. At 30% Zn, single-phase α with annealing twins after recrystallization. At 40% Zn and air cooling, a Widmanstätten α+β 'basket weave'. At 45% Zn with rapid cooling, uniform β retained. The Al-Si system shows how solidification rate controls microstructure scale: slow casting yields coarse brittle Si plates, while rapid solidification refines Si to nanoscale spheroids. Cu-Sn bronze illustrates dendritic coring - Sn concentration gradient frozen by rapid solidification. Ti-6Al-4V shows Widmanstätten α plates in a β matrix via a precise crystallographic precipitation transformation, giving high strength with limited ductility.

C. Conclusions

1. Cooling rate controls scale and morphology. Slow cooling allows equilibrium phases to grow large (coarse pearlite, large graphite flakes, large dendrites). Fast cooling refines the structure (fine pearlite, Widmanstätten plates) or suppresses equilibrium transformations entirely (martensite, retained β phase).  · 

2. Microstructure directly controls properties. Martensite is hard and brittle; coarse pearlite is softer and tougher; graphite flakes act as internal cracks reducing ductility; annealing twins in brass signal a well-recrystallised ductile condition; Widmanstätten Ti-6Al-4V is strong but needs post-processing for adequate ductility.

3. Composition determines which phases are possible. Carbon content positions a steel in the Fe-C diagram (hypoeutectoid, eutectoid, hypereutectoid, or cast iron). Alloying elements shift the TTT curve, modify hardenability, and stabilise or suppress phases. Zinc content in brass determines α/β balance.  · 

D. Answers to Questions

1. What are the common phases observed in the ferrous alloy micrographs you collected?
Common phases observed in ferrous alloy micrographs include ferrite, pearlite, cementite, martensite, bainite, and austenite. Ferrite appears as light regions and is relatively soft and ductile. Pearlite consists of alternating lamellae of ferrite and cementite. Cementite is a hard iron carbide phase. Martensite forms as a needle like or lath like structure after rapid cooling. Bainite appears as a feathery or acicular structure formed at intermediate cooling rates. Austenite is stable at high temperature and may be retained at room temperature in some steels.

2. How does the carbon content influence the microstructure of steel?
Carbon content strongly influences the microstructure of steel. Low carbon steels contain mostly ferrite with small amounts of pearlite, resulting in high ductility and low strength. Medium carbon steels show a balance of ferrite and pearlite, increasing strength and hardness. High carbon steels contain large amounts of pearlite and cementite, leading to higher hardness and lower ductility. In eutectoid steel, the structure is fully pearlitic. As carbon increases beyond the eutectoid composition, proeutectoid cementite forms along grain boundaries.

3. What are the typical microstructural features of the non ferrous alloys you selected?
Typical microstructural features of non ferrous alloys depend on the system. Aluminum alloys often show an aluminum matrix with fine precipitates of alloying elements. Copper alloys may display equiaxed grains in single phase solid solutions or a mixture of alpha and beta phases. Magnesium alloys frequently exhibit dendritic alpha magnesium and intermetallic phases along grain boundaries. Grain size, precipitate distribution, and phase morphology vary depending on composition and processing.

4. How do heat treatments alter the microstructures of the alloys presented?
Heat treatments significantly alter microstructure. Annealing promotes recrystallization and grain growth, reducing dislocation density and producing equiaxed grains. Normalizing refines grain size and results in a more uniform pearlitic structure in steels. Quenching transforms austenite into martensite, increasing hardness. Tempering reduces brittleness by allowing controlled precipitation and partial decomposition of martensite. In precipitation hardening alloys, solution treatment followed by aging forms fine coherent precipitates that strengthen the matrix.

5. Based on your research, how do microstructural features relate to the mechanical properties of the alloys?
Microstructural features directly influence mechanical properties. Fine grains increase strength according to the Hall Petch relationship. A higher fraction of hard phases such as martensite or cementite increases hardness and tensile strength but reduces ductility. Uniform precipitate distribution improves strength through precipitation hardening. Grain boundary phases may either strengthen or weaken the alloy depending on their morphology and distribution.

6. Explain the importance of magnification in micrograph analysis.
Magnification determines the scale at which features can be resolved. Low magnification reveals overall grain structure and phase distribution. High magnification allows observation of lamellae spacing in pearlite, precipitates, or dislocation structures. Without proper magnification, critical features may be misinterpreted or overlooked.

7. What are some challenges in comparing micrographs from different sources?
Challenges in comparing micrographs from different sources include differences in etching technique, magnification, sample preparation, and imaging equipment. Variations in lighting and contrast can change the apparent morphology of phases. Inconsistent scale bars or missing compositional information also make accurate comparison difficult.

8. How does the processing history of a material affect its microstructure?
Processing history strongly affects microstructure. Mechanical working such as rolling introduces elongated grains and high dislocation density. Casting produces dendritic structures. Heat treatment alters phase distribution and grain size. Cooling rate determines whether phases such as pearlite, bainite, or martensite form. Thus, microstructure reflects the thermal and mechanical history of the material.

9. What are the benefits of creating a literature based compendium of microstructures?
Creating a literature based compendium of microstructures allows systematic comparison of alloys and treatments. It helps identify trends between composition, processing, and properties. It also serves as a reference for phase identification and supports deeper understanding of structure property relationships.

10. How can phase diagrams be used to interpret the microstructures observed in the literature?
Phase diagrams are used to interpret which phases are thermodynamically stable at given compositions and temperatures. By locating the alloy composition and temperature on the diagram, one can anticipate phase fractions and transformations, which aids in explaining the observed microstructures and validating literature observations.
